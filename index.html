The Key Risk
Cyclicality — real but overstated. Revenue fell 61% during COVID (CIQ: €5,570m → €2,174m). EBITDA went from €2,231m to €159m. That is the number the market remembers. But COVID required governments to ban flying — a once-in-a-century event. In every other downturn (GFC, SARS, 9/11, oil shocks), global air travel dipped single digits and recovered within 12-18 months. Normal cyclicality for this business means a 5-15% revenue dip in a bad year, not 61%.

The franchise does not leak value during a crisis — it just temporarily processes less of it. Zero airlines left PSS during COVID. The moat held perfectly. And pricing power partially offsets volume declines: revenue per passenger boarded grew 4.7% and revenue per booking grew 5.0% in FY2025 (Amadeus FY2025 Results), even as macro conditions deteriorated. PSS costs are sub-1% of airline revenue — airlines cut fuel hedges, routes, and headcount before they think about PSS. Over the medium term, the monopoly position means Amadeus recovers everything once volumes normalise.

The mitigating observation: COVID permanently impaired Sabre ($3.6bn net debt per CIQ, negative cash from operations), reducing the number of viable third-party PSS alternatives. The competitive position is stronger post-crisis than before it.

§ 2.1 - What PSS Actually Does

What PSS Does

PSS is not a product airlines can choose to use or not. It is the system that makes an airline operational. Without PSS, no flight departs.
Reservations (PNR)
Creates and manages passenger name records. Every booking - direct, OTA, GDS, corporate - flows through this system. The PNR is the single source of truth for who is on which flight. Amadeus processes hundreds of millions of bookings per year through this system alone.
Inventory & Revenue Management
Controls seat availability and pricing for every flight. The system decides, in real-time, which fare classes are open, how many seats remain, and what price to display. Revenue management algorithms optimize across millions of booking possibilities per day.
Departure Control (DCS)
Manages check-in, boarding, gate assignment, and passenger processing. DCS prints boarding passes, validates documents, manages upgrades, and reconciles the manifest. If DCS goes down, no one boards. Every airline operation depends on this system functioning flawlessly, millions of times daily.
Ticketing & Revenue Accounting
Issues e-tickets, manages fare rules, handles interline settlement. Revenue accounting tracks every transaction across codeshare partners, alliance members, and interline agreements. The complexity is significant - a single itinerary can involve 4 airlines, 3 currencies, and 12 fare components.

Why These Four Systems Cannot Be Separated

Reservations, inventory, DCS, and ticketing interact in real-time, millions of times per day. A booking in Reservations instantly updates Inventory (one fewer seat). Inventory feeds Revenue Management (price the remaining seats higher). DCS pulls from Reservations at check-in. Ticketing validates against all three. Switching one system without the others is technically impossible - the data formats, APIs, and business logic are deeply intertwined after decades of customization. Switching ALL FOUR simultaneously means running dual systems for 18–36 months: double the cost, double the failure risk, double the IT staff.

§ 2.2 - Switching Cost Forensics

Switching Costs

Switching Barrier What It Means in Practice Severity
Migration Timeline Full PSS migration takes 18–36 months of parallel running. During this period, the airline operates two complete reservation/DCS/inventory systems simultaneously. Staff must be trained on both. Every airport, every check-in kiosk, every gate reader must be reconfigured. The airline cannot do a "big bang" cutover - it must migrate route by route, station by station. EXTREME
Operational Failure Risk If the new PSS fails during cutover, the airline cannot check in passengers, cannot issue boarding passes, cannot manage revenue. Flights are grounded or operated manually (paper manifests). The reputational and financial cost of even a single day of system failure exceeds years of PSS fees. British Airways' 2017 data centre power failure cost £80M+ in a single weekend - illustrating the catastrophic cost of airline IT outages, even outside of PSS migration. EXISTENTIAL
Customisation Depth Over 10–20+ years, each airline has built hundreds of custom business rules, fare structures, loyalty integrations, and codeshare agreements into their PSS instance. This accumulated business logic is deeply customised and tightly coupled to the PSS instance, creating migration complexity comparable to legacy banking system replacements. EXTREME
Cost vs. Value PSS fees are estimated at <1% of airline revenue for most carriers. Amadeus Air IT FY2024 revenue was €2,204.7m on 2,164m passengers boarded, or approximately €1.02 per passenger. A mid-size airline processing 20m passengers on €5bn revenue would pay approximately €20m, or 0.4% of revenue. A large carrier (80m passengers, €30bn revenue) would pay approximately €80m, or 0.27%. Note: Air IT includes Airport IT and Expert Services, so pure PSS cost per passenger is lower than €1.02. For context, fuel is 25-29% of airline costs and labour is 28% (IATA, 2024-2026). The cost of switching vastly exceeds any potential PSS savings.

§ 2.4 - Pricing Power Evidence

Pricing Power

Revenue per Booking Growth
4–6% CAGR
Amadeus has consistently grown revenue per booking at 4-6% annually for the last two years (FY2024: +6.0%, FY2025: +4.7% at constant currency). This is pure pricing power. Airlines absorb the increases because PSS costs are immaterial relative to total operations (<1% of revenue for most carriers) and switching is impossible. At approximately €1.02 per passenger boarded (Air IT FY2024 revenue €2,204.7m / 2,164m PB), PSS costs are estimated at well below 1% of airline revenue for most carriers.
Contract Structure
10–15yr terms
PSS contracts run 10–15 years with automatic escalators (CPI + spread). Renewal rates are very high. Departures in the last decade have been rare and mostly linked to merger consolidation (LATAM) or regional factors (Russian sanctions). The contract provides revenue visibility spanning over a decade. For a concentrated long-hold fund, this is the ideal compounding engine: predictable, growing, and protected by contract for longer than most fund lives.

Amadeus vs. Comparable Software

SAP ERP: Runs back-office systems for most large global enterprises. Switching takes 2-5 years. S/4HANA migration forces reinvestment, not departure. Same.

TransDigm: 90% sole-source aerospace parts. FAA recertification required for alternatives (18-36 months). Pricing power repeatedly demonstrated despite regulatory scrutiny. Same.

Every year that Sabre cannot invest in PSS product development (constrained by $3.6bn net debt and negative free cash flow), the technology gap with Amadeus widens and the effective switching cost for airlines increases — even without any action by Amadeus.

Growth Rate, Revenue Model, and Multiple Selection

The comps grow revenue at 15–25% p.a. Amadeus Air IT grows ~4–7%. The comps earn subscription revenue (collected regardless of usage); Amadeus earns transaction revenue (per passenger boarded — COVID: −61%). These are real differences, and the primary reason the SOTP applies 18–25× rather than the full 22–36× comp range.

Where Amadeus scores higher is on durability: the comps face genuine competitive risk (Salesforce re-entering Veeva's market, Microsoft vs ServiceNow, new gov-tech entrants vs Tyler). Amadeus PSS has fewer than 10 voluntary departures in 20 years. The 18× low end represents a 50% discount to Veeva at 22×. Amadeus's own pre-COVID multiple was 15–16× when the market fully understood the transaction-based model. Caveat: the pre-COVID multiple was set in a near-zero rate environment; some compression from 15-16× to 9.5× may reflect rate normalisation.

Sensitivity: At 14× implied EBITDA (a 36% discount to the lowest comp), Air IT = €15.5bn — still 67% of total EV, implying remaining segments at 6.3×. Even at heavily discounted multiples, the SOTP gap persists.

§ 3.5 - Analyst Coverage

Analyst Coverage

Who Covers Amadeus Today
Travel & leisure analysts at JPMorgan, Goldman, Morgan Stanley. They compare Amadeus to Booking Holdings, Expedia, IAG, TUI. They use sector EV/EBITDA (12–15×) and apply it uniformly. They write about "post-COVID travel recovery" and "NDC disruption risk." They treat PSS as a travel business.
Who SHOULD Cover PSS
Enterprise software analysts covering SAP, Oracle, ServiceNow, Veeva. They understand mission-critical infrastructure with very low voluntary churn. They apply 22-36x EV/EBITDA to mission-critical software platforms with contractual revenue. If a single Tier 1 software analyst published a SOTP note on Amadeus, the re-rating conversation would begin.
The Catalyst
Software analyst initiation, improved segment disclosure, or shareholder engagement on the structure.

Segment Disclosure and Capital Allocation

Management does not report PSS separately within the Air IT Solutions segment. Group-level reporting limits the market's ability to value PSS independently. Cash flows from Air IT fund R&D across all segments, including the lower-margin Hospitality business.

Is the Market Right or Wrong?

The case that 9.5× is correct: Transaction revenue may deserve a permanent structural discount to subscription revenue — COVID demonstrated that Amadeus's earnings can fall 61% in a single year, something that never happened to ServiceNow, Tyler Tech, or Veeva. A 4-7% grower may not warrant software multiples regardless of lock-in. The segments are related (all travel technology), making bundled valuation appropriate. And the pre-COVID multiple of 15-16× was set in a zero-rate environment with no demonstrated evidence of cyclical vulnerability; post-COVID repricing to 9.5× may reflect a rational reassessment, not an overshoot.

The case that 9.5× is too low: Amadeus's pre-COVID multiple of 15–16× was applied by a market that already understood the transaction-based model, moderate growth, and segment bundling — and still paid 60-70% more than today. The competitive position is stronger now than in 2019 (Sabre financially impaired). Organic EBITDA has exceeded 2019 levels. Three factors may explain the persistent discount without validating it: (1) the travel-tech classification is self-reinforcing — software analysts don't cover IBEX travel stocks; (2) the institutional register is passive by composition; (3) the discount was narrower at 15-16× and only became potentially actionable after compression to 9.5×.

This document does assert a view: 9.5× is too low. The business earns 17-20% ROIC through the cycle, has fewer than 10 voluntary customer departures in 20 years, and its only third-party competitor is financially impaired. A 9.5× blended multiple prices in a level of structural risk that the observable data does not support. The pre-COVID market — which fully understood the transaction-based model, the growth rate, and the segment mix — paid 15-16×. The franchise is stronger now than it was then. The entry multiple is 40% lower.

Why the Stock Underperformed the Business

The 10-year price chart looks bad because it is bad — and that's why the entry is attractive.

§ 7.2 - The Thesis (3-Minute Version)

Thesis Summary

The compounding case. Amadeus generates €2,448m EBITDA (FY2025 per S&P CIQ), earns 17-20% ROIC through the cycle, and trades at 9.5× EV/EBITDA — below its own historical range of 10-16×. EBITDA growth: +7.3% FY2024→2025 (CIQ). Dividend yield: 2.9% at entry. Buybacks: €613m/€362m/€1,336m in FY2023-25 (CIQ). At scale since FY2023 only. FY2025 total returns exceeded UFCF by €564m. Net debt: 0.9× EBITDA. 10-year return model (§ 4.2): base case assumes 5% EBITDA growth, no re-rating (exit 9.5×), no buybacks → ~8% CAGR. Adding buybacks: ~11%. Adding partial re-rating to 13×: ~15%. Full re-rating (SOTP closes at 16×): ~18%. The base case is unkillable — it assumes nothing beyond conservative growth and dividends.

The franchise. The Altéa Passenger Service System runs reservations, ticketing, departure control, and revenue management for 200+ airlines. Fewer than 10 voluntary PSS departures in 20 years (author's review of public records). 18-36 month migration timeline (sourced: Southwest 3 years per PR Newswire; JAL 3 years per JAL press release). Estimated PSS cost: <1% of airline revenue for most carriers (derived from Amadeus FY2024 filing). The only third-party alternative — Sabre — carries $3.6bn net debt (CIQ) with negative cash from operations.

The entry price. 9.5× is too low. The pre-COVID market paid 15-16× for this exact business with this exact revenue model. The franchise is stronger now (Sabre impaired, EBITDA has exceeded 2019 levels, competitive position improved). A history-based SOTP — applying Amadeus's own peak multiple to the highest-quality segment — produces €58-68/share (+18-41%). A comp-based SOTP produces €66-97/share (see § 3). The compounding case does not require any re-rating: the buyback programme alone adds 35-46% to per-share terminal value over a decade.

The key risk. Revenue fell 61% during COVID (CIQ). EBITDA collapsed from €2,231m to €159m. This is transaction revenue, not subscription. The franchise held — no airline left — but the cash flows didn't. The stress case models this honestly.

§ 7.3 - Key Questions and Answers

Key Questions and Answers

Question My Answer
"What reprices it? Conglomerate discounts can persist." Three paths: (1) Software analyst initiation - one SOTP note from a Tier 1 bank starts the conversation. (2) Capital return - if management reallocates fortress cash flows to buybacks, the mechanical re-rating begins. (3) Shareholder engagement - an engaged shareholder's SOTP letter would be the first active voice on a fully passive register. But even without catalyst: 9–12% CAGR from organic compounding is a defensible hold at €48.6. The re-rating is upside optionality, not the base case.
"COVID proved the revenue is cyclical. How durable is the franchise?" Revenue fell 61% during COVID. No airline left PSS during the crisis. Sabre came out with $3.6bn net debt, -92% price return. Competitive landscape is better now than 2019.
"What about NDC and AI? Aren't they disintermediating the GDS?" Yes - but that's Tier 3, not Tier 1. NDC disrupts the distribution layer (how bookings reach airlines). PSS is the processing layer (how bookings are executed). AI agents and NDC pipes still need PSS to check in passengers, issue tickets, manage inventory, and settle interline transactions. My SOTP already prices Distribution at 8-12x implied EBITDA - the fear is in the multiple. The fortress is upstream, unaffected, and arguably benefits from NDC complexity (more integration middleware, more PSS value).
"Why at €48.6? Why now?" The blended multiple has compressed from 15–16× (2019) to 9.5× - partly justified (COVID proved cyclicality) and partly mispricing (post-COVID franchise is stronger, and the stock has overshot to the downside). At €48.6, the SOTP gap is roughly 67% at midpoint. Base case at 13× delivers +42% on current earnings plus 10-14% annual compounding. A true stress scenario (mid-cycle earnings at trough multiple) implies ~15-20% downside — real but recoverable.
"Why did Sabre and Travelport not make the cut?" Sabre: predominantly a distribution business with a smaller and shrinking airline IT segment. Sold hotel IT to TPG for $1.1bn ($960m net proceeds) to service debt. $3.6bn net debt, $536m EBITDA (Sabre-adjusted basis), interest expense approaches or exceeds operating income. Travelport: pure GDS distribution, no PSS, private, heavily leveraged. EBITDA not publicly disclosed.
"The oil shock is crushing airline volumes. Why won't this break the franchise?" Volume drops hurt short-term revenue — Amadeus earns per passenger boarded, not per contract. But the franchise does not leak value during a crisis — it just temporarily processes less of it. Three things limit the damage: (1) Pricing power partially offsets volume declines — revenue per passenger boarded grew 4.7% and revenue per booking grew 5.0% in FY2025 (Amadeus FY2025 Results), even as macro conditions deteriorated. The monopoly position allows Amadeus to push price when volume softens. (2) PSS costs are sub-1% of airline revenue — airlines cut fuel hedges, routes, and headcount before they even consider PSS. (3) COVID was the extreme stress test — revenue fell 61%, zero airlines left, the franchise recovered to above 2019 EBITDA within 5 years, and the only competitor (Sabre) came out permanently impaired. Normal downturns produce single-digit revenue dips, not 61%. The market is pricing in a level of cyclical risk that the observable evidence outside of COVID does not support.
"What about new cloud-native PSS competitors?" Several startups and initiatives exist (e.g. IBS Software's iFly Res, Bravo Passenger Solutions). None has won a major full-service carrier migration. The barrier is not technology — it's the 18-36 month migration risk, the need for global airport coverage, interline settlement capability, and regulatory compliance across 190+ countries. Building a PSS is possible; convincing an airline to bet its operations on an unproven system is the moat. This is the same dynamic that protects SAP: competitors exist, but migration risk keeps incumbents in place.
