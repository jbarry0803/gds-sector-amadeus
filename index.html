§ 2.1 - What PSS Actually Does

The Operating System of Global Aviation

PSS is not a product airlines can choose to use or not. It is the system that makes an airline operational. Without PSS, no flight departs.
Reservations (PNR)
Creates and manages passenger name records. Every booking - direct, OTA, GDS, corporate - flows through this system. The PNR is the single source of truth for who is on which flight. Amadeus processes ~690M bookings/year through this system alone.
Inventory & Revenue Management
Controls seat availability and pricing for every flight. The system decides, in real-time, which fare classes are open, how many seats remain, and what price to display. Revenue management algorithms optimize across millions of booking possibilities per day. This is where airlines make money.
Departure Control (DCS)
Manages check-in, boarding, gate assignment, and passenger processing. DCS prints boarding passes, validates documents, manages upgrades, and reconciles the manifest. If DCS goes down, no one boards. Every airline operation depends on this system functioning flawlessly, millions of times daily.
Ticketing & Revenue Accounting
Issues e-tickets, manages fare rules, handles interline settlement. Revenue accounting tracks every transaction across codeshare partners, alliance members, and interline agreements. The complexity is staggering - a single itinerary can involve 4 airlines, 3 currencies, and 12 fare components.

Why These Four Systems Cannot Be Separated

Reservations, inventory, DCS, and ticketing interact in real-time, millions of times per day. A booking in Reservations instantly updates Inventory (one fewer seat). Inventory feeds Revenue Management (price the remaining seats higher). DCS pulls from Reservations at check-in. Ticketing validates against all three. Switching one system without the others is technically impossible - the data formats, APIs, and business logic are deeply intertwined after decades of customization. Switching ALL FOUR simultaneously means running dual systems for 18–36 months: double the cost, double the failure risk, double the IT staff. No airline CEO will risk grounding their fleet to save 2% on IT costs. This is not a negotiating position. It is an operational reality.

§ 3.3 - Why the Market Gets It Wrong

The Analyst Coverage Problem

Who Covers Amadeus Today
Travel & leisure analysts at JPMorgan, Goldman, Morgan Stanley. They compare Amadeus to Booking Holdings, Expedia, IAG, TUI. They use sector EV/EBITDA (12–15×) and apply it uniformly. They write about "post-COVID travel recovery" and "NDC disruption risk." They treat PSS as a travel business.
Who SHOULD Cover PSS
Enterprise software analysts covering SAP, Oracle, ServiceNow, Verint. They understand mission-critical infrastructure with zero churn. They apply 35–40× to monopoly software platforms with contractual revenue. If a single Tier 1 software analyst published a SOTP note on Amadeus, the re-rating conversation would begin.
The Catalyst
Three paths to re-coverage:
1) A software analyst initiates coverage (most likely trigger)
2) Amadeus management improves segment disclosure (unlikely without pressure)
3) An activist/engaged shareholder publishes a SOTP letter - the first voice the passive register has ever heard. This is where comes in.

The Conglomerate Structure Serves Management, Not Shareholders

Is the opaque reporting accidental or deliberate? Almost certainly the latter - but not for shareholder value reasons. The bundled structure serves management's interests: Luis Maroto runs a €20.9bn company with three segments. If PSS were separated or transparently disclosed, IT Solutions and Hospitality would be exposed as mediocre standalone entities that cannot justify premium compensation. The bundled structure lets management cross-subsidise weaker segments with PSS cash flows, obscuring the capital allocation question: "Why are you investing the monopoly's cash flows into competitive businesses?" The solution is structural separation or shareholder engagement.

"Catalyst: three paths. Software analyst re-coverage - one Tier 1 initiation changes the conversation. Capital return policy - fortress cash flows funding buybacks instead of subsidising hospitality. Or shareholder engagement - even at 0.5–1%, could be the first active voice on a register that's entirely passive. The SOTP letter is the catalyst.

"Without catalyst: 9–12% CAGR from organic compounding - fortress volume growth plus pricing power. With partial re-rating: 14–20%. At €48.6, even the bear case - no re-rating, slow growth - delivers mid-single-digit returns. The margin of safety at this price is real, and it wasn't there at €68."

§ 7.3 - Key Questions and Answers

Key Questions and Answers

Anticipated questions on the thesis, with sourced answers.
Question My Answer
"What reprices it? Conglomerate discounts can persist." Three paths: (1) Software analyst initiation - one SOTP note from a Tier 1 bank starts the conversation. (2) Capital return - if management reallocates fortress cash flows to buybacks, the mechanical re-rating begins. (3) Shareholder engagement - an engaged shareholder's SOTP letter would be the first active voice on a fully passive register. But even without catalyst: 9–12% CAGR from organic compounding is a defensible hold at €48.6. The re-rating is upside optionality, not the base case.
"COVID proved the revenue is cyclical. How is this a monopoly?" The franchise is not cyclical - the revenue is. Zero airlines left PSS during COVID. Zero. The monopoly held structurally while revenue fell 61%. The distinction matters: Amadeus is a toll road where traffic is variable but the toll is permanent. More importantly, COVID permanently impaired Sabre (~$4bn net debt, –92% TSR over 10 years). The competitive landscape is better now than in 2019. The monopoly strengthened through the crisis.
"What about NDC and AI? Aren't they disintermediating the GDS?" Yes - but that's Tier 3, not Tier 1. NDC disrupts the distribution layer (how bookings reach airlines). PSS is the processing layer (how bookings are executed). AI agents and NDC pipes still need PSS to check in passengers, issue tickets, manage inventory, and settle interline transactions. My SOTP already prices Distribution at 11–16× EBIT - the fear is in the multiple. The fortress is upstream, unaffected, and arguably benefits from NDC complexity (more integration middleware, more PSS value).
"Why at €48.6? Why now?" The blended multiple has compressed from 15–16× (2019) to ~9.5× - partly justified (COVID proved cyclicality) and partly mispricing (post-COVID franchise is stronger, and the stock has overshot to the downside). At €48.6, the SOTP gap is roughly 100% - more than double the stock price to fair value. Base case without any re-rating delivers 9–12% CAGR from compounding alone. This is no longer a 'fair entry for a great franchise' - it's a genuinely cheap entry for a monopoly. Amadeus at 9.5× with ~100% SOTP upside is approaching deep value territory for a monopoly business.
"How does this compare to FIH?" FIH (the Falklands case) has stronger crisis resilience (essential services, no COVID revenue collapse). But at €48.6, Amadeus now matches or exceeds FIH on entry discount: the SOTP gap is very wide at this entry price. Amadeus's mispricing is structural (conglomerate discount) - it can be addressed through analyst coverage, shareholder engagement, or management action. The key advantage over FIH: at Amadeus's scale, there are levers to pull.
"Why did Sabre and Travelport not make the cut?" Neither of them owns the monopoly. Sabre's Travel Solutions is ~79% GDS distribution - the commodity pipe - with only ~21% airline IT, and that piece is shrinking as carriers de-migrate off SabreSonic. They sold their hotel IT business to TPG in 2025 for $965m and used the proceeds to pay down debt, but they're still carrying ~$4bn net debt against ~$536m EBITDA (interest expense of $448m still exceeds operating income). They had to sell one of their better assets just to service the debt. The equity is a levered option on a GDS recovery, not a franchise. Travelport is worse: almost pure-play GDS distribution, no PSS at all, private with no public financials, and $3.95bn of debt on ~$260m EBITDA. Both are distribution businesses with leverage. Amadeus is the only one where the core product is mission-critical infrastructure with zero churn. That's the entire difference.
