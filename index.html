§ 2.1 - What PSS Actually Does

What PSS Does

PSS is not a product airlines can choose to use or not. It is the system that makes an airline operational. Without PSS, no flight departs.
Reservations (PNR)
Creates and manages passenger name records. Every booking - direct, OTA, GDS, corporate - flows through this system. The PNR is the single source of truth for who is on which flight. Amadeus processes ~690M bookings/year through this system alone.
Inventory & Revenue Management
Controls seat availability and pricing for every flight. The system decides, in real-time, which fare classes are open, how many seats remain, and what price to display. Revenue management algorithms optimize across millions of booking possibilities per day.
Departure Control (DCS)
Manages check-in, boarding, gate assignment, and passenger processing. DCS prints boarding passes, validates documents, manages upgrades, and reconciles the manifest. If DCS goes down, no one boards. Every airline operation depends on this system functioning flawlessly, millions of times daily.
Ticketing & Revenue Accounting
Issues e-tickets, manages fare rules, handles interline settlement. Revenue accounting tracks every transaction across codeshare partners, alliance members, and interline agreements. The complexity is staggering - a single itinerary can involve 4 airlines, 3 currencies, and 12 fare components.

Why These Four Systems Cannot Be Separated

Reservations, inventory, DCS, and ticketing interact in real-time, millions of times per day. A booking in Reservations instantly updates Inventory (one fewer seat). Inventory feeds Revenue Management (price the remaining seats higher). DCS pulls from Reservations at check-in. Ticketing validates against all three. Switching one system without the others is technically impossible - the data formats, APIs, and business logic are deeply intertwined after decades of customization. Switching ALL FOUR simultaneously means running dual systems for 18–36 months: double the cost, double the failure risk, double the IT staff.

§ 2.2 - Switching Cost Forensics

Switching Costs

Switching Barrier What It Means in Practice Severity
Migration Timeline Full PSS migration takes 18–36 months of parallel running. During this period, the airline operates two complete reservation/DCS/inventory systems simultaneously. Staff must be trained on both. Every airport, every check-in kiosk, every gate reader must be reconfigured. The airline cannot do a "big bang" cutover - it must migrate route by route, station by station. EXTREME
Operational Failure Risk If the new PSS fails during cutover, the airline cannot check in passengers, cannot issue boarding passes, cannot manage revenue. Flights are grounded or operated manually (paper manifests). The reputational and financial cost of even a single day of system failure exceeds years of PSS fees. British Airways' 2017 IT failure cost £80M+ in a single weekend. EXISTENTIAL
Customisation Depth Over 10–20+ years, each airline has built hundreds of custom business rules, fare structures, loyalty integrations, and codeshare agreements into their PSS instance. This accumulated business logic is undocumented, untransferable, and irreproducible - the same dynamic that keeps banks on legacy mainframes. EXTREME
Cost vs. Value PSS fees represent <2% of airline revenue. The cost of switching (migration project: $50–200M, operational risk, 2+ years of dual running) vastly exceeds any savings from a cheaper provider. STRUCTURAL
Competitive Alternatives Sabre: ~$4bn net debt, –92% TSR over 10 years, negative free cash flow, functionally uninvestable as a long-term PSS partner. Travelport: Private, small, limited airline portfolio. In-house: Only feasible for the largest carriers (Lufthansa, Delta) who built proprietary systems over many years. For 95% of airlines, Amadeus is the only viable option. NO ALTERNATIVE

§ 2.4 - Pricing Power Evidence

Pricing Power

Revenue per Booking Growth
3–5% CAGR
Amadeus has consistently grown revenue per booking at 3–5% annually - above inflation - for over a decade. This is pure pricing power. Airlines absorb the increases because PSS costs are immaterial relative to total operations (<2% of revenue) and switching is impossible. Amadeus Air IT revenue of €2.2bn across 150+ airlines equates to less than 0.25% of global airline industry revenue (~$965bn per IATA).
Contract Structure
10–15yr terms
PSS contracts run 10–15 years with automatic escalators (CPI + spread). Renewal rates are functionally 100% - no airline has ever failed to renew. The contract provides revenue visibility spanning over a decade. For a concentrated long-hold fund like , this is the ideal compounding engine: predictable, growing, and protected by contract for longer than most fund lives.

Amadeus vs. Comparable Software

SAP ERP: Runs back-office of 87% of Fortune 500. Switching takes 2–5 years, costs $50–500M. S/4HANA migration forces reinvestment, not departure. Same.

TransDigm: 90% sole-source aerospace parts. FAA recertification required for alternatives (18–36mo, $10M+). Pentagon investigated pricing - found no alternative. Same.

§ 5.1 - The Structural Parallel

FIH Group and Amadeus: Same Pattern, Different Scale

FIH Group (AIM: FIH)
The franchise: Falkland Islands Company. 175-year-old sole provider of essential services (retail, shipping agency, property) for 3,500 people, 8,000 miles from any competitor.

The conglomerate wrapper: FIH also owns Momart (art logistics) and until March 2026 owned Portsmouth Harbour Ferry (PHFC). Two loss-making sub-segments (FBS Construction, Momart Exhibitions) suppressed the group multiple to ~4x EBITDA.

The catalyst: PHFC sold to Collins River / Uber Boat at 12.6x pre-IFRS 16 EBITDA in March 2026. Strategic buyer paid 12.6x. Remaining portfolio (FIC + Momart) available at ~4.7x implied.

Market cap: £33.5m. The gap between PHFC's sale multiple (12.6x) and the rump entry (4.7x) is the mispricing.
Amadeus IT (BME: AMS)
The franchise: Altea PSS. Runs reservations, ticketing, departure control, revenue management for 150+ airlines. No documented PSS departure on public record. 70.9% contribution margin.

The conglomerate wrapper: Amadeus also operates GDS distribution (48% of revenue, lower margin, NDC-exposed) and Hospitality & Other (16% of revenue, competitive). Travel analysts apply a single blended multiple to the whole group.

The catalyst: No structural separation has occurred yet. The SOTP gap is quantifiable but unrealised. An engagement pointing out that Air IT alone could be worth more than total EV would be the first active voice on a fully passive register.

Market cap: €20.9bn. The gap between where comparable mission-critical software trades (26-41x) and the blended group multiple (9.5x) is the mispricing.

§ 7.2 - The Thesis (3-Minute Version)

Thesis Summary

Amadeus is three businesses under one listing, priced at 9.5× blended EV/EBITDA.

"Air IT contains the PSS franchise. The Altéa Passenger Service System runs reservations, ticketing, departure control, and revenue management for over 150 airlines. There is no documented case of an airline departing Amadeus PSS on public record. Switching takes 18–36 months of dual-system running, costs $50–200 million, and risks grounding your fleet. PSS fees are less than 2% of airline revenue. The cost of failure is existential. No CEO will take that trade. This is infrastructure lock-in - the same dynamics that keep banks on legacy systems for decades.

"The Air IT Solutions segment - which contains Altea PSS, Navitaire, Airport IT and Expert Services - generates €1,563m of contribution at a 70.9% margin. That is 47% of total segment contribution on 36% of revenue. Comparable mission-critical software trades at 26-41x on contribution. Even at 18–25× (discounted for cyclicality and non-PSS sub-segments), Air IT alone is worth €28–39 billion. Total enterprise value today is €23.2bn.

"The second business - Hospitality and Payments - is durable. €249m EBIT, growing, worth €5–6 billion.

"The second largest segment - Air Distribution - is the one the market fears. €1,392m contribution at a 47.3% margin, but facing structural disruption from NDC and direct airline distribution. The fear is valid. But even pricing distribution conservatively at 8–12× contribution, it adds €11–17 billion. The market's error isn't overvaluing distribution - it's undervaluing Air IT by applying the same blended multiple to everything.

"Midpoint SOTP: €97 per share versus €48.6 today. ~100% upside. Low case: €77 (+59%). High case: €116 (+139%). At €48.6, this is no longer a 'fair price for a great franchise' - the stock has moved into genuine deep-value territory.

"The weakness I need to own: the revenue is cyclical. COVID took revenue down 61% and ROE to –16.7%. The franchise held - zero churn - but the cash flows didn't. Amadeus has a dominant franchise with cyclical revenue. The mitigation: COVID permanently impaired Sabre (~$4bn net debt, negative FCF, –92% TSR), eliminating the only credible alternative. The competitive position is stronger post-crisis than before it.

"Catalyst: three paths. Software analyst re-coverage - one Tier 1 initiation changes the conversation. Capital return policy - fortress cash flows funding buybacks instead of subsidising hospitality. Or shareholder engagement - even at 0.5–1%, could be the first active voice on a register that's entirely passive. The SOTP letter is the catalyst.

"Without catalyst: 9–12% CAGR from organic compounding - fortress volume growth plus pricing power. With partial re-rating: 14–20%. At €48.6, even the bear case - no re-rating, slow growth - delivers mid-single-digit returns. The margin of safety at this price is real, and it wasn't there at €68."

§ 7.3 - Key Questions and Answers

Key Questions and Answers

Question My Answer
"What reprices it? Conglomerate discounts can persist." Three paths: (1) Software analyst initiation - one SOTP note from a Tier 1 bank starts the conversation. (2) Capital return - if management reallocates fortress cash flows to buybacks, the mechanical re-rating begins. (3) Shareholder engagement - an engaged shareholder's SOTP letter would be the first active voice on a fully passive register. But even without catalyst: 9–12% CAGR from organic compounding is a defensible hold at €48.6. The re-rating is upside optionality, not the base case.
"COVID proved the revenue is cyclical. How durable is the franchise?" Revenue fell 61% during COVID. Zero airlines left PSS. The franchise held structurally while revenue fell 61%. The distinction matters: Amadeus is a toll road where traffic is variable but the toll is permanent. More importantly, COVID permanently impaired Sabre (~$4bn net debt, –92% TSR over 10 years). The competitive landscape is better now than in 2019. The competitive position strengthened through the crisis.
"What about NDC and AI? Aren't they disintermediating the GDS?" Yes - but that's Tier 3, not Tier 1. NDC disrupts the distribution layer (how bookings reach airlines). PSS is the processing layer (how bookings are executed). AI agents and NDC pipes still need PSS to check in passengers, issue tickets, manage inventory, and settle interline transactions. My SOTP already prices Distribution at 11–16× EBIT - the fear is in the multiple. The fortress is upstream, unaffected, and arguably benefits from NDC complexity (more integration middleware, more PSS value).
"Why at €48.6? Why now?" The blended multiple has compressed from 15–16× (2019) to ~9.5× - partly justified (COVID proved cyclicality) and partly mispricing (post-COVID franchise is stronger, and the stock has overshot to the downside). At €48.6, the SOTP gap is roughly 100% - more than double the stock price to fair value. Base case without any re-rating delivers 9–12% CAGR from compounding alone.
"How does this compare to FIH?" FIH has stronger crisis resilience (essential services, no COVID revenue drop). Amadeus's SOTP gap is wider at this price. Mispricing is structural (conglomerate discount).
"Why did Sabre and Travelport not make the cut?" Sabre: ~79% distribution, ~21% airline IT (shrinking). Sold hotel IT to TPG for $965m to service debt. ~$4bn net debt, $536m EBITDA, interest ($448m) exceeds operating income. Travelport: pure GDS distribution, no PSS, private, $3.95bn debt on ~$260m EBITDA.
